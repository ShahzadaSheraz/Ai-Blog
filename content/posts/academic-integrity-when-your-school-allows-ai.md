Some institutions now allow limited generative AI in certain courses; others prohibit it entirely. “Allowed sometimes” is the hardest regime because the boundary moves assignment by assignment. This article gives a practical integrity system: disclosure, draft retention, verification, and collaboration hygiene.

## Read the assignment, not just the syllabus

Policies can differ per project. Look for: permitted stages (brainstorm only?), citation requirements for AI assistance, logging requirements, and bans on uploading third-party copyrighted readings.

If ambiguous, email the instructor with a concrete example: “Is it acceptable to use AI to generate practice questions from my notes?” Written answers reduce misunderstandings.

## Disclosure that is specific and short

Good disclosure states:

- Which tool category you used (e.g., large language model for outlining).
- What you used it for (outline, grammar suggestions, code refactor suggestions).
- What you did *not* use it for (final calculations, primary research).

Avoid vague disclaimers (“AI may have been used”) that do not help instructors evaluate your work.

## Keep drafts and intermediate artifacts

Retain:

- Your first handwritten or typed outline.
- Version history timestamps if available.
- Source PDFs and reading notes you relied on.

If integrity is questioned, you can reconstruct how ideas developed. This habit also improves learning.

## Verification is non-negotiable

Even when AI is allowed, hallucinated citations are still academic misconduct risk. Every factual claim needs a traceable source you personally checked—same standard as in [AI writing assistants for essays and research](/blog/ai-writing-assistants-for-essays-and-research).

## Group projects: define boundaries early

Agree in writing: who uses which tools, how shared drafts are produced, and how each member’s contributions are tracked. Unequal AI use can create fairness disputes.

## Exams: assume stricter rules

Exam integrity is usually tighter than homework integrity. Treat exam periods like a different policy zone—see [ethical exam prep with AI support](/blog/ethical-exam-prep-with-ai-support).

## When AI is allowed, quality still judges you

Instructors may grade harder on originality and depth if AI levels the playing field on grammar. Invest in stronger examples and sharper analysis—skills emphasized in [durable skills online earners should build](/blog/durable-skills-online-earners-should-build).

## Documentation templates you can paste into an appendix

Some courses allow an “AI use appendix” with: tools used, dates, prompts categories (not necessarily full logs), and a statement that citations were verified manually. Ask whether your instructor wants screenshots or just a narrative. Pair with writing-stage guidance in [AI writing assistants for essays and research](/blog/ai-writing-assistants-for-essays-and-research).

## Collaboration contracts for team projects

Agree in writing: who runs AI tools, how shared drafts merge, and how each member proves contribution. If one teammate uses AI heavily, others should not be surprised at grading time.

## FAQ

**If AI is allowed, can I use it on exams?** Usually no—treat exams separately. **Does disclosure hurt my grade?** Not if your work stands on its own; ask instructors how disclosure is evaluated.

## Plagiarism vs assistance: a practical distinction

Plagiarism is presenting someone else’s work as your own without attribution. Assistance is support that leaves authorship and understanding clearly yours. The boundary shifts by assignment type. When uncertain, ask for a worked example of acceptable assistance from your instructor.

## International students: visa and work-study constraints

Earning online may interact with visa rules; integrity includes legal compliance, not only classroom rules. When in doubt, consult your international student office rather than informal online advice.

## Recordkeeping for scholarships and honors programs

Some programs ask whether AI was used in application essays. Keep drafts and disclosure statements consistent across submissions. If you also build a portfolio, align narrative integrity with [online portfolio sites with AI and no-code tools](/blog/online-portfolio-sites-with-ai-and-no-code-tools).

## Long workshop: write a two-hundred-word integrity memo

Draft a memo to yourself describing exactly how you will use AI this semester: allowed stages, forbidden stages, disclosure wording, and file retention rules. Ask a peer to critique clarity. This memo becomes your default when temptation rises during deadlines.

## Long workshop: compare two drafts for provenance

Keep a “human-only outline” and an “AI-assisted outline” for the same assignment (if policy allows experimentation). Compare where each leads. If the AI-assisted outline drifts from sources faster, that is a warning sign about your prompting style.

## Long workshop: instructor office hour script

Prepare three questions: one about policy ambiguity, one about citation expectations, one about collaboration boundaries. Write them without AI first, then tighten wording. The habit trains professional communication beyond school.

## Composite case study: “Taylor” navigates mixed policies across courses

Taylor’s CS course encouraged AI for debugging while Taylor’s humanities seminar banned generative drafting. Taylor maintained separate folders and disclosure templates per course and avoided cross-pasting drafts between them. Taylor kept version timestamps to show how arguments evolved, which made integrity conversations factual rather than emotional.

When Taylor began freelancing, the same separation discipline prevented accidentally pasting client material into homework tools. Integrity systems scale across contexts if you build them as habits, not one-off statements. ## Quick recap box

Ask early, disclose specifically, keep drafts, verify citations, and treat exams as a stricter regime than homework. Integrity is less about moral theater and more about repeatable documentation that protects you when stress peaks.

Closing note: when two authorities conflict—syllabus versus forum advice—believe the syllabus and get written clarification. That single habit prevents ninety percent of integrity panic.

One more habit: keep instructor emails short, specific, and polite—clarity reduces back-and-forth and protects relationships when stress is high.

## Takeaways

- Confirm per assignment; get ambiguous cases in writing.
- Disclose specifically; keep drafts and notes.
- Verify facts and citations personally.
- Separate exam rules from homework rules.

Integrity systems reduce anxiety: you are not hoping nobody asks—you can show your work.
