Remote language work—translation, transcription, subtitling—can fit flexible student schedules. AI has shifted the market: machine translation is stronger, clients expect faster turnaround, and “post-editing” is now a common job category. This guide explains what beginners should expect, how quality is judged, and where AI helps versus hurts.

## Machine translation is a first draft, not a deliverable

Professional workflows often use MT plus human post-editing (MTPE). Your value is judgment: register, idioms, cultural references, consistency of terminology, and fidelity to intent.

If you are still building language skill, invest in drills from [AI language learning tools that actually help](/blog/ai-language-learning-tools-that-actually-help).

## Subtitling is timing + reading speed, not just translation

Good subtitles respect reading speed, line breaks, and speaker changes. AI tools can generate drafts, but you must watch the video while editing—no skipping playback.

## Tooling and file formats

Learn common formats (SRT, VTT) and platform quirks (YouTube captions, streaming guidelines). Small technical mistakes lose gigs.

## Ethics and rights

Only work on content you have rights to handle. Do not upload confidential media to random online translators.

## Building a portfolio without violating NDAs

Create spec work: translate a short public domain paragraph and show before/after commentary explaining decisions. Or subtitle a royalty-free clip with permission.

## Pricing: charge for expertise, not keystrokes

Clients who want cheap MT paste will price shop forever. Position around accuracy, niche terminology, and fast communication.

## Terminology consistency: the hidden quality bar

Clients notice inconsistent translations of the same term across episodes or documents. Maintain a glossary sheet: source term, chosen translation, forbidden alternatives, context notes. AI can propose glossary entries, but a human must approve them—especially for branded terms.

## Post-editing speed without sacrificing accuracy

Use timed passes: first pass for meaning, second pass for register and idioms, third pass for formatting. Track recurring errors in a personal log; speed comes from pattern elimination, not typing faster.

## FAQ

**Do I need certification?** Not always, but tests and credentials can help early. **Is raw MT output acceptable for subtitles?** Rarely; timing and line breaks need human review.

## Client scenarios: news clips vs corporate training

News clips demand speed and fidelity to speaker intent; corporate training videos demand consistent terminology and brand voice. AI drafts help in both, but the risk profile differs. In corporate work, a mistranslated compliance line can create real liability—budget extra review time and keep a signed glossary from the client. In news, nuance and idiom matter; literal translations sound amateur fast.

## Rate anchors: how beginners think about cents per minute

Some marketplaces quote per minute of video or per word of source text. Track your effective hourly rate after revisions. If revisions balloon because the draft MT was poor, your “high” per-minute rate becomes a low hourly reality. Over time, push toward clients who value accuracy and pay for a second reviewer when needed.

## Networking without spamming

Cold outreach works better when you reference a specific gap you can solve—subtitle cleanup for a channel with multilingual audiences, for example.

## Long workshop: post-edit a machine draft in timed passes

Take a 300-word MT paragraph in your pair language and post-edit it in three timed passes: five minutes for meaning fidelity, five minutes for register and idioms, five minutes for punctuation and consistency with a mini glossary you wrote beforehand. Log errors you missed after a mentor or peer review. Repeat weekly; speed emerges from error reduction, not rushing.

## Long workshop: subtitle a ninety-second clip manually

Disable auto-timing tools for the first pass. Type captions while listening at reduced speed. Then compare against an AI draft and reconcile differences. You will learn which shortcuts the AI takes—line breaks that hide emphasis, reading-speed violations that feel fine until you watch on a phone.

## Long workshop: build a ten-term glossary for a niche you enjoy

Pick a niche you actually watch: skincare science, cycling tech, indie games. Create ten terms with definitions and “do not translate as” notes. This becomes a portfolio artifact and trains the judgment clients pay for.

## Quick recap box

Ship work that survives playback on a phone, keep glossaries for consistency, and treat machine translation as a draft that you own end-to-end. Speed matters, but repeat clients come from fewer embarrassing errors and calmer communication when deadlines slip.

Closing note: when deadlines slip, communicate early with a new ETA and a mitigation plan—panic silence destroys trust faster than imperfect subtitles.

Keep a “style sheet” for names and places so spelling stays consistent across episodes.

## Takeaways

- Expect MTPE workflows; learn to edit, not just translate from scratch.
- Subtitle with the video running; respect reading speed.
- Build a portfolio with public materials and explained choices.
- Avoid processing content you are not authorized to share.

AI changed the speed curve; it did not remove responsibility. The translators who thrive treat models as draft engines and own the final word.
